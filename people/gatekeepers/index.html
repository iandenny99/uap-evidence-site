Gatekeepers (US & UK)

This page highlights figures and institutions frequently cited by journalists, lawmakers, and advocates as obstructing or slowing UAP transparency. Inclusion here is based on public reporting and official records noted below. It’s presented for accountability and as a starting point for legitimate journalistic inquiry.

Important: Allegations are summarized with links to primary sources or reputable coverage. Readers should evaluate the materials directly. If you’re named here and wish to provide a statement, contact us.

United States — Named Individuals

Rep. Michael R. Turner (R-OH)

Why listed: As a senior House intelligence leader, Turner has been repeatedly cited by advocates and media as opposing expansive UAP disclosure provisions, with critics arguing this helped produce watered-down outcomes in final NDAA language.

  • Funding context: documented support from defense-related industries (see OpenSecrets industry breakdowns).
  • Sources: Newsweek overview on suppression claims; OpenSecrets profile/industries.

Rep. Mike D. Rogers (R-AL)

Why listed: As a top Armed Services figure, Rogers has been associated in reporting and advocacy with resistance to strong UAP disclosure mechanisms in NDAA negotiations.

  • Funding context: substantial receipts from defense-sector PACs/individuals.

Sen. Roger Wicker (R-MS)

Why listed: Identified by numerous advocates and press as a central opponent in Senate negotiations that curtailed or excluded stronger UAP Disclosure Act provisions. The final NDAA language was significantly diluted compared with earlier proposals.

OpenSecrets – Wicker summary · Defense aerospace PACs · Popular Mechanics – UAPDA watered down · Contact · @SenatorWicker

Glenn Gaffney (former CIA Director of Science & Technology)

Why listed: A document submitted to the House Oversight Committee (Nov 13, 2024) asserts that CIA/DS&T, during a meeting with Gaffney while in that role, disapproved transferring UAP-related materials to AAWSAP/Bigelow facilities—effectively keeping custody within CIA.

United Kingdom — Institutional Gatekeeping

Unlike the U.S., the UK has no prominently named individual “gatekeepers.” Instead, long-standing institutional policy functions as the gate: the Ministry of Defence ceased investigating UFO/UAP reports in 2009 and has repeatedly stated there are no plans to re-establish a dedicated team. That stance keeps the issue below the political/media radar.

  • Parliamentary answer (Dec 2024): MoD ceased UAP work in 2009; no new team planned
  • House of Lords debate (June 2021)
  • Janes: UK govt says UAPs pose no military threat (Dec 2024)

Implication for journalists: In the UK, focus FOI requests and scrutiny on policy, procurement, and inter-agency coordination rather than named blockers. Ask: who receives UK military UAP incident reports today? What is the escalation path? Which offices interface with allies on UAP data?
